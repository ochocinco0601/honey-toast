# Contributing — Landing Paths and Scope

This page describes what practitioner contributions look like and where they land in the BOK's content-state discipline. If you're modifying the BOK's *structure* — adding or changing Knowledge Areas, Topics, Instruments, or Perspectives — see [Maintaining the BOK](maintaining-the-bok.md) instead.

The BOK grows when real work touches topics. Not a separate authoring project.

**Note on procedure.** This page explains the contribution model — it does not yet prescribe a specific submission workflow (where to put content, who reviews, how it moves from staged candidate to page). The procedural flow will form as real contributions arrive and reveal the operational shape. For now, contributions move through direct conversation with the practice's stewards — whoever maintains your organization's copy of this body of knowledge.

## Three landing paths

**Current** — content practitioner-actionable from the page alone. Lands directly in the KA's Domain Knowledge or Practices section.

**Seeded** — candidate frame with cited prior art; needs validation or more data points. Held by the stewards as a staged candidate — indexed by target KA, candidacy grade (how close it is to landing), and content hook — until validation promotes it.

**Missing** — topic should exist, doesn't. Structure declares it. Content State section flags it. *"This is missing"* is useful information, not failure.

## What gets contributed

- Candidate topic content from real work
- Validated prior art mappings (named framework + citation)
- Cross-topic signals — which KAs reality keeps pointing at

## What doesn't

- Speculative frames without prior-art provenance
- Content from reading other BOKs without real-use validation
- Navigation infrastructure without content backing

## Structural signals

If a topic has no clean home — or KA boundaries don't fit what real work is producing — that's a structural signal. Surface it. The taxonomy is an opinion that can be revised. Persistent patterns that don't fit = evidence the structure needs iteration. Structural change itself follows a defined process — see [Maintaining the BOK](maintaining-the-bok.md) Part VIII.
